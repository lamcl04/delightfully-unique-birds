Birds: A Scientific Wonder

What's So Wonderful About Birds?

Birds are an extremely diverse class of animals with a wide variety of behaviors, appearances, and adaptations. Some birds are common and cute, like the Tufted Titmouse, while others are lesser known and have a more jarring appearance, like the Cassowary. Some birds are flighted, like the Red Bellied Woodpecker, and some can't fly, like the Adélie penguin. Some are seed eaters or insect eaters, ground nesters or tree nesters, but all belong to the class Aves.

Acorn Woodpecker

The Acorn Woodpecker is a type of woodpecker that lives in small colonies in the western United States and Central America. These woodpeckers are monogamous and lay three to seven eggs once per year. Their colonies share the effort of raising the young, taking turns incubating the eggs for about 14 days. After hatching, baby Acorn Woodpeckers will stay in the nest for approximately 32 days before fledging. They live in small groups in oak trees, which also function as storage for their food. These woodpeckers drill small holes into trees and store their acorns in said holes. One Acorn Woodpecker can store thousands of acorns and other nuts per year! Bearded Vulture

Bearded Vultures live in the mountainous regions of Africa, Europe, and Asia, nesting in cliffs and caves. They are solitary birds, only spending time around others when nesting or scavenging for food. These vultures lay one to two eggs per clutch and are monogamous. After laying, Bearded Vultures will incubate the eggs for up to 60 days, and the babies will stay in the nest until they are 15 or 16 weeks old. Bearded Vultures, unlike other vultures, are not bald. This is due to their unique diet of primarily bones! They have learned to break bones into easily consumable pieces by flying them to great heights and dropping them. Sometimes, however, they will eat large bones whole. They are able to do this because of their large size – they have a wingspan of 7 to 9 feet and weigh up to 17 pounds.

Kākāpō

The Kākāpō, native to New Zealand, is the only flightless parrot in the world, weighing as much as 9 pounds at only 24 inches tall! They are critically endangered due to habitat loss, hunting, and introduced predators such as mice and cats. They lay one to four eggs per clutch, incubate for 30 days, and care for the chicks until 6 months old (even though the chicks fledge at around 10 weeks old). The male Kākāpō do not help incubate or raise the young. The diet of the Kākāpō consists of plants and fruit.

Cassowary

The Cassowary is a large flightless bird that lives in the tropical forests and wetlands of Australia and New Guinea. They are the second heaviest bird in the world, weighing up to 175 pounds and standing at an average height of 5.6 feet! They are able to run at speeds up to 31 miles per hour, jump up to 7 feet vertically, and swim. The Cassowary is a solitary species, with only the male raising and incubating the young. The eggs of the Cassowary are about 5 inches long –– you can see an egg in slide 3 of the slideshow. Despite their scary appearance and large size, these birds eat a diet of exclusively fruit.

Useful links for birdwatching and bird identification

These are some useful sites for birdwatching and bird identification. All sites below are created by the Cornell Lab of Ornithology.

https://www.allaboutbirds.org/guide/search

This site is a comprehensive guide to birds of North America. It also features articles on bird identification skills, birdsong identification skills, and links to optional paid bird identification courses.

https://merlin.allaboutbirds.org/

Part of All About Birds, this site is an identification tool. You can record and upload unidentified birdsong or photos of birds to pin down what species of bird is in the image/recording.

https://ebird.org/home

This site is essentially a digital fieldnotes book. eBird allows you to track your birding routes, the birds you saw, and to create a 'life-list' (a running list of birds you've seen in your life). It also allows you to see the birding trips that other people in your area have completed.
